\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:chap5_intro}
% ============================================================

The previous chapter introduced a segmentation-guided
attention mechanism, evaluated on a preliminary dataset
covering four departments of the Île-de-France region,
using very high resolution (VHR) optical imagery and
segmentation annotations derived from BD TOPO. While this
dataset already demonstrated the value of rich, structured
annotations for RSVQA, it remained limited in two important
respects. First, it relied on a single image modality: VHR
optical imagery provides fine spatial detail but no
information beyond the visible spectrum, and no broader
spatial context beyond the $200\,\text{m} \times
200\,\text{m}$ extent of each patch. Second, its geographic
coverage was restricted to a single, predominantly urban
region, limiting the diversity of landscapes and the
question types that could be meaningfully asked.

Remote sensing is inherently a multimodal domain. Different
sensors capture complementary information about the same
physical scene: very high resolution optical imagery
provides fine-grained spatial detail; multispectral imagery,
such as that acquired by the Sentinel-2 mission, captures
spectral signatures across visible, near-infrared, and
short-wave infrared bands that characterize ground materials
and vegetation health; and synthetic aperture radar (SAR),
such as Sentinel-1, provides information independent of
cloud cover and illumination, highlighting man-made
structures and surface roughness through backscatter
intensity. Crucially, these modalities differ not only in
spectral content but also in spatial resolution and
coverage: while VHR imagery captures fine detail over a
small extent, multispectral and SAR data naturally provide
broader spatial context at coarser resolution. A VQA system
that can jointly exploit this multimodal, multi-resolution
information has the potential to answer a much richer range
of questions than one restricted to a single modality and a
single spatial extent.

Despite this potential, existing RSVQA datasets remain
overwhelmingly single-modality. As summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:chap5_datasets_comparison}, datasets such as
RSVQA-LR, RSVQA-HR~\cite{lobry2020rsvqa}, and
RSVQAxBEN~\cite{lobry2021rsvqaben} rely exclusively on
optical RGB imagery, whether from Sentinel-2 or
high-resolution airborne sensors. While SAR has been
explored for specific RSVQA sub-tasks~\cite{tosato2024sar},
no existing dataset combines very high resolution optical
imagery, multispectral data, and SAR within a unified
question-answering framework. This gap is not a
matter of dataset construction convenience: it reflects a
genuinely open question for the computer vision
community, namely how to design models capable of jointly
reasoning over inputs with heterogeneous spectral content,
spatial resolution, and physical interpretation.

This chapter addresses this gap through two contributions.
First, we introduce TAMMI (Text And Multi-Modal Imagery), a
large-scale dataset extending the preliminary benchmark of
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention} along three axes:
geographic coverage is extended from four to six
departments across three contrasting regions of
Metropolitan France (urban Île-de-France, mountainous
(Haute-Savoie), and maritime (Hérault); imagery is extended
from VHR-only to a triplet of VHR, multispectral, and SAR
modalities, geographically co-registered around each VHR
patch; and the question taxonomy is extended from 9 to 21
question types across five categories, drawing on five
geographic databases (BD TOPO, flood risk data, urban
zoning, land cover, and mountain area data). The resulting
dataset contains 282,852 images and over 3.1 million
question-answer pairs. Second, we propose MM-RSVQA
(Multi-modal Multi-resolution RSVQA), a baseline model built
on VisualBERT~\cite{li2019visualbert} that fuses features
from all three modalities with the question through a
trainable transformer-based fusion process, and we conduct
an ablation study quantifying the contribution of each
modality to the VQA task.

The remainder of this chapter is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:chap5_rw} reviews related work on
multimodal dataset construction and multi-resolution RSVQA.
Section~\ref{sec:chap5_dataset} describes the TAMMI dataset,
including its image modalities, question taxonomy, and
balancing procedure, and positions it relative to the
partial dataset of Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention} and other
existing RSVQA benchmarks. Section~\ref{sec:chap5_method}
presents the MM-RSVQA architecture.
Section~\ref{sec:chap5_experiments} reports experimental
results and an ablation study on modality contributions.
Section~\ref{sec:chap5_seg_attention_multimodal} describes
an earlier direction that was explored and abandoned, in
which the segmentation-guided attention mechanism of
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention} was extended to the
multimodal setting, and analyses why it was replaced by
the final architecture.
Section~\ref{sec:chap5_conclusion} concludes with limitations
and perspectives.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:chap5_rw}
The related work covered in Chapter~\ref{chap:background}
addresses the broader foundations of this chapter: general
VQA architectures, RS-VQA datasets, and vision-language
transformer models such as VisualBERT and LXMERT at the
conceptual level. Attention mechanisms and their application
to RSVQA are covered in Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}.
This section therefore focuses on two aspects that are
specific to the contributions of this chapter: the
construction of multimodal datasets in remote sensing beyond
the VQA setting, and the question of spatial context and
multi-resolution in RSVQA.

\subsection{Multimodal Dataset Construction in Remote Sensing}
\label{sec:chap5_rw_multimodal}

Outside of VQA, the construction of multimodal remote
sensing datasets combining optical and SAR imagery is a
well-established practice.
\textcite{sumbul2021bigearthnetmm} introduce BigEarthNet-MM,
extending the original Sentinel-2-only BigEarthNet
archive~\textcite{sumbul2019bigearthnet} with co-registered
Sentinel-1 SAR patches, annotated with CORINE Land Cover
labels, to support multimodal classification and retrieval.
This dataset illustrates both the value and the difficulty
of multimodal RS data: co-registering optical and SAR
acquisitions at scale requires careful handling of
differing acquisition geometries, revisit times, and
preprocessing pipelines, but the resulting joint
representations consistently improve downstream
classification performance compared to single-modality
inputs.

Within RSVQA specifically, multimodality has so far been
explored only in narrow settings.
\textcite{tosato2024sar} investigate whether SAR imagery can
improve RSVQA performance when combined with optical data,
focusing on a subset of question types and a single pair of
co-registered modalities. While this work demonstrates that
SAR contributes complementary information to RSVQA, it does
not extend to a full dataset spanning multiple modalities,
question types, and geographic regions, nor does it
incorporate very high resolution imagery alongside
satellite-based modalities. The dataset introduced in this
chapter builds directly on this finding, extending it to a
three-modality setting (VHR, multispectral, and SAR) at the
scale required for general-purpose RSVQA benchmarking.

\subsection{Multi-Resolution and Context-Aware RSVQA}
\label{sec:chap5_rw_context}

A related but distinct line of work concerns the role of
spatial context in RSVQA. Most existing RSVQA datasets pose
questions exclusively about the content of a single image
patch, implicitly assuming that all information required to
answer a question is contained within that patch's spatial
extent. However, many natural questions about a location (its administrative region, its broader land cover context,
or its proximity to mountainous or coastal areas) require
information at a coarser spatial scale than a single VHR
patch can provide.

The dataset introduced in this chapter addresses this by
design: multispectral and SAR patches are extracted at a
spatial extent independently configurable from the VHR
patch, allowing the same geographic location to be described
at multiple resolutions simultaneously. This formalizes
multi-resolution context as a first-class property of the
dataset, rather than an incidental consequence of sensor
characteristics. To the best of our knowledge, this is the
first RSVQA dataset to explicitly decouple the spatial extent
of contextual modalities from that of the primary imagery,
enabling questions whose answers depend on information at a
scale broader than the directly imaged area, such as
administrative or land cover classification questions
discussed in Section~\ref{sec:chap5_dataset_qa}.

\subsection{Image Modalities}
\label{sec:chap5_modalities}

TAMMI associates each geographical location with a triplet
of co-registered images from three complementary sensors,
described below.

\textbf{Very high resolution orthophotos (BDOrtho).}
BDOrtho is a database of aerial optical imagery produced by
the French National Geographic Institute (IGN), providing
RGB orthophotos at 20\,cm resolution over the whole of
Metropolitan France, updated approximately every three
years. As in Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, these images
constitute the primary, highest-resolution modality of the
dataset. For consistency across departments, the most
recent available acquisition is used for each: 2020 for
department 74 (Haute-Savoie), and 2021 for departments
34, 75, 92, 93, and 94. The acquisition year for each
department is also used to select temporally consistent
acquisitions for the other two modalities, ensuring that the
three images of a given triplet describe the same physical
scene at approximately the same point in time.

\textbf{Multispectral imagery (Sentinel-2).} Sentinel-2,
part of the European Union's Copernicus
program~\cite{sentinel2}, acquires multispectral imagery
across 13 spectral bands spanning the visible, near-infrared
(NIR), and short-wave infrared (SWIR) regions, at spatial
resolutions of 10\,m, 20\,m, or 60\,m depending on the band.
With a constellation of two satellites (Sentinel-2A and
Sentinel-2B), the revisit time over a given location is
approximately five days. Sentinel-2 data is distributed at
two processing levels: Level-1C (L1C), providing
top-of-atmosphere (TOA) reflectance, and Level-2A (L2A),
providing surface reflectance after atmospheric correction.
Since BDOrtho images are acquired under clear-sky conditions
and require no atmospheric correction, and SAR imagery is
unaffected by atmospheric conditions altogether, L1C products
are used throughout TAMMI to maintain consistency across
modalities. Only images with cloud coverage below 3\% are
retained. Of the 13 available bands, the 10 bands at 10\,m
and 20\,m resolution are used, discarding the three 60\,m
atmospheric bands which carry limited information about
ground-level land cover.

\textbf{Synthetic Aperture Radar imagery (Sentinel-1).}
Sentinel-1, also part of the Copernicus
program~\cite{sentinel1}, provides SAR imagery with a
revisit time of approximately six days over Europe. SAR
acquisition is based on active sensing: the satellite emits
radar pulses and measures the backscattered signal, a process
unaffected by cloud cover or solar illumination. TAMMI uses
data acquired in Interferometric Wide (IW) swath mode, the
primary acquisition mode over land, with a resolution of
5\,m in range (across the satellite's trajectory) and 20\,m
in azimuth (along the trajectory). IW acquisitions are
divided into three sub-swaths, each composed of sequentially
acquired tiles (\textit{bursts}) with inter-burst overlap;
Single Look Complex (SLC) Level-1 products represent these
bursts within a single image separated by black bands.
Sentinel-1 acquires data in two polarization
channels (VV and VH), which respond differently to
surface roughness and material composition, providing
complementary physical information about the scene. TAMMI
uses the amplitude of the SLC Level-1 images for both
polarization channels. Prior to use, SAR images undergo a dedicated preprocessing
pipeline jointly involving the SAR and VHR data, described
below.

\begin{enumerate}

    \item \textbf{Position projection.} The algorithm
    of~\cite{weissgerber2022labsar} is used to project
    the geographic position of the center of each VHR
    patch into the SAR image coordinate space. Altitude
    must be included in this projection due to the
    geometric distortions inherent in SAR acquisitions,
    particularly layover effects. Latitude and longitude
    are extracted from the VHR image metadata; altitude is
    retrieved from the IGN altimetry web
    service~\cite{ign_alti} and stored alongside the
    geographic coordinates in a per-patch JSON file
    (\texttt{latlonalt.json}).

    \item \textbf{Swath selection.} Sentinel-1 SLC images
    are divided into three sub-swaths. The projection
    algorithm is applied to the metadata of each sub-swath;
    the sub-swath for which the algorithm returns a valid
    pixel position is selected for that VHR patch.

    \item \textbf{Debursting.} Within each sub-swath, the
    SLC image comprises multiple overlapping bursts stored
    in a single file separated by black bands. These black
    bands and inter-burst overlaps are removed using the
    SLC metadata and by comparing consecutive bursts,
    producing a spatially continuous image. The debursted
    images are provided with the dataset. The projection
    algorithm is updated to return the correct position in
    the debursted coordinate system, and the resulting
    position and selected swath are stored in the patch
    JSON file.

    \item \textbf{Patch extraction and dB conversion.}
    When a patch of size $L_\text{SAR} \times L_\text{SAR}$
    is extracted at runtime, the two polarimetric channels
    (VV and VH) are converted to decibels and their ratio
    VV/VH is computed, yielding a three-channel SAR patch.

    \item \textbf{Tail-value elimination.} A tail-value
    removal procedure is applied independently to each of
    the three channels, using statistics computed over the
    full dataset to identify and clip extreme backscatter
    values that would otherwise dominate the dynamic range.

\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Geographic extent.} TAMMI extends the geographic
coverage of Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention} from four to
six departments, grouped into three contrasting regions of
Metropolitan France, illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:chap5_geo}. The Île-de-France region
(departments 75, 92, 93, and 94), already used in
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, is predominantly urban.
Haute-Savoie (department 74) is a mountainous region in the
French Alps, and Hérault (department 34) is a maritime
region on the Mediterranean coast. This selection deliberately
broadens the range of land cover types represented in the
dataset: while questions about administrative regions, water
bodies, or vegetation are meaningful but visually homogeneous
in an urban context, the addition of mountainous and
maritime landscapes introduces new object classes (mountain
ranges, coastlines) and new question types
(Section~\ref{sec:chap5_dataset_qa}) that would be absent in Île-de-France alone.

\textbf{VHR patch extraction.} As in
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, VHR tiles of
$25{,}000 \times 25{,}000$ pixels are divided into patches
$p_\text{VHR}$ of $1{,}000 \times 1{,}000$ pixels, each
covering $200\,\text{m} \times 200\,\text{m}$ on the ground.
Each such patch defines the geographical extent
$E_{p_\text{VHR}}$ used both to query geographic databases
for question generation (Section~\ref{sec:chap5_dataset_qa})
and to determine the corresponding multispectral and SAR
patches.

\textbf{Multispectral and SAR patch extraction.} Unlike the
VHR patch, whose extent is fixed by construction, the
multispectral patch $p_\text{MS}$ and SAR patch $p_\text{SAR}$
are extracted at a spatial extent of $L_\text{MS} \times
L_\text{MS}$ and $L_\text{SAR} \times L_\text{SAR}$ pixels
respectively, where $L_\text{MS}$ and $L_\text{SAR}$ are
user-defined parameters specifying how much spatial context
is provided by each modality. This is a deliberate design
choice: because multispectral and SAR data are available at
coarser resolution but cover a wider area for the same number
of pixels, they can provide the model with information about
the broader surroundings of the VHR patch (for instance,
whether the area lies near a coastline, within an urban
agglomeration, or close to a mountain range) without
requiring this information to be visible within the
$200\,\text{m} \times 200\,\text{m}$ extent of
$p_\text{VHR}$ itself. The patches $p_\text{MS}$ and
$p_\text{SAR}$ are cropped dynamically at execution time,
allowing $L_\text{MS}$ and $L_\text{SAR}$ to be adjusted
without regenerating the dataset.

\textbf{Spatial co-registration.} To align the three
modalities, the geographic position (latitude, longitude,
and altitude) of the central pixel of each $p_\text{VHR}$ is
computed. A multispectral tile centered on this position is
retrieved by converting the geographic coordinates to pixel
coordinates in the geocoded Sentinel-2 product. For SAR
imagery, which is not natively geocoded, the projection of
the central pixel position into the SAR image's pixel space
is performed using the method
of~\cite{weissgerber2022labsar}, originally developed for
SAR/InSAR coregistration in high-mountain regions, a
context particularly relevant given the inclusion of
Haute-Savoie in TAMMI's geographic extent.

\subsection{Question/Answer Taxonomy}
\label{sec:chap5_dataset_qa}

TAMMI's question/answer pairs are constructed automatically
from five geographic databases, each contributing distinct
object classes and question types:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BD TOPO}~\cite{bdtopo}: the vectorial
    description of the French territory used throughout this
    thesis, providing generic geographic objects (buildings,
    roads, water areas) as well as specific entities (museums,
    monuments, religious buildings);
    \item \textbf{TRI} (\textit{Territoires à Risque
    important d'Inondation}), the flood risk
    database from Géorisques~\cite{georisques_tri},
    providing management zoning, floodable area zoning, and
    water level zoning for areas at significant flood risk;
    \item \textbf{BU20} (\textit{Base des Unités urbaines
    2020}), the urban unit database from
    INSEE~\cite{insee_bu20}, providing urban zoning of French
    cities based on construction continuity and population;
    \item \textbf{CORINE Land Cover (CLC) 2018}, the
    pan-European land cover and land use database managed by
    the European Environment Agency, used at its most detailed
    level (Level-3, 44 classes)~\cite{clc2018};
    \item \textbf{European Mountain Areas (EMA)}, a database
    from the European Environment
    Agency~\cite{ema} providing the geometry, area, and name
    of European mountain ranges.
\end{itemize}

For a given VHR patch $p_\text{VHR}$, the collection of
geo-located objects $\mathbf{o}^C = \{o_i^C\}$ present in its
geographic extent $E_{p_\text{VHR}}$ is retrieved for each
relevant class $C$ across these five sources. Questions are
then generated from these object collections according to
five question categories, totaling 21 question types,
summarized in Table~\ref{tab:chap5_qtypes}.

Compared to the nine question types used in
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, which correspond to a
subset of the Quantity, Relational, and Comparison types
above (all drawn from BD TOPO), TAMMI more than doubles the
question taxonomy. The additional types fall into two groups.
First, several types (mountain presence, mountain range name,
flood presence, flood level, flood type) are only meaningful
given TAMMI's broader geographic extent: mountains and flood
zones are largely absent from the Île-de-France departments
used in Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}. Second, the
classification questions about department, region, urban
classification, and land cover (CLC) require information at a
spatial scale beyond that of a single VHR patch, and are
answered using the broader geographic context made available
through TAMMI's multi-resolution design
(Section~\ref{sec:chap5_triplets}).
\label{sec:chap5_balancing}

As discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:llm_augmentation}, an
unbalanced distribution of questions and answers introduces
language biases that allow models to perform well by
exploiting statistical shortcuts rather than genuine visual
understanding~\cite{chappuis2023curse}. TAMMI's balancing
procedure operates jointly at two levels: within each
department, and across the dataset as a whole.

For each VHR patch $p_\text{VHR}$, ten candidate questions per
type are randomly generated from the available object
collections. Questions are then selected from this candidate
pool according to four criteria: (1) an equal number of
questions is retained for each question type; (2) the number
of questions per department is proportional to the number of
VHR patches in that department; (3) the distribution of
answers within each question type is balanced; and (4) no
more than 50 question/answer pairs are retained per patch.

For question types with a fixed number of possible answers
$N_A$ (\textit{e.g.}, Presence, with $N_A = 2$ for
\textit{yes}/\textit{no}), the maximum number of questions per
answer is set to:

\begin{equation}
    N_{Q,A} = \frac{N_P}{\min(N_A, 10)}
\end{equation}

where $N_P$ is the number of VHR patches in the department.
For Location questions, answers are binned into a $3 \times 3$
spatial grid, giving $N_A = 9$. For question types with free
numerical answers (\textit{e.g.}, Area, Count, Distance), the
maximum number of questions sharing the same numerical answer
$x$ is instead:

\begin{equation}
    N_{Q,A} = \frac{N_P}{\log(x + 3)}
\end{equation}

where the offset of 3 was chosen empirically. This formulation
caps the frequency of any single numerical value while
allowing rarer (typically larger) values to appear with
proportionally higher relative frequency, counteracting the
natural long-tailed distribution of quantities such as area or
count in geographic data.

Department and Region questions require special handling: for
a given department, the answer to a Department question is
always the same (\textit{e.g.}, every patch in department 75
yields the answer ``Paris''), and similarly for Region. Left
unconstrained, this would make these question types trivially
predictable from the answer distribution alone. To preserve a
balanced answer distribution at the dataset level despite this
per-department constraint, the number of occurrences of each
Department answer is capped at 2,473 (the number of VHR
patches in Paris, department 75, the smallest department in
the dataset), and each Region answer is capped at 16,274
(the total number of patches in Île-de-France, the smallest
region). This ensures that no single department or region
answer dominates the overall answer distribution, even though
it is deterministic within any given department.

Two observations follow from this comparison. First, TAMMI is

distinct spatial resolutions, whereas all other datasets
(including the partial dataset of

Second, TAMMI substantially increases both the number of

listed datasets except RSVQAxBEN, whose far larger answer
count of 26,875 stems primarily from a small number of
numerical question types (areas and counts over BigEarthNet
patches) rather than from question type diversity,
RSVQAxBEN provides only 2 question types, against TAMMI's 21.
This combination of modality diversity, question type
diversity, and answer diversity is the central methodological
contribution of TAMMI relative to prior RSVQA datasets.

\subsection{Dataset Splitting}
\label{sec:chap5_splitting}

As in Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, the dataset is split
at the image level into training, validation, and test sets
with a 60\%/20\%/20\% proportion. Splitting at the image level,
rather than at the question level, ensures that no VHR
patch (and therefore no geographic location) appears in more
than one split, preventing information leakage between
training and evaluation through spatially correlated
questions about the same location.

\section{Methodology: MM-RSVQA}
\label{sec:chap5_method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Overview}
\label{sec:chap5_method_overview}

We propose MM-RSVQA (Multi-modal Multi-resolution Remote
Sensing Visual Question Answering), a baseline model for
the multimodal RSVQA task introduced by TAMMI. The model
takes as input a triplet of images $(p_\text{VHR},
p_\text{MS}, p_\text{SAR})$ and a natural language question
$q$, and predicts an answer from a closed vocabulary of
$k = 1{,}000$ classes. It consists of three components:
a multi-modal feature extraction module, a
transformer-based fusion module, and a prediction head.
The architecture is illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:chap5_arch}.

\subsection{Multi-Modal Feature Extraction}
\label{sec:chap5_method_features}

Each of the three image modalities is processed by a
separate, independently pre-trained feature extractor,
chosen to match the spectral and spatial characteristics
of each modality.

\textbf{VHR encoder.} Visual features $f_\text{VHR}$ are
extracted from $p_\text{VHR}$ using a ResNet-152
model~\cite{he2016resnet} pre-trained on ImageNet,
consistent with the encoder-based RSVQA paradigm discussed
in Chapter~\ref{chap:llm_augmentation}. The use of a
deeper ResNet-152 here, compared to the ResNet-50 used in
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, reflects a difference in
focus: Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention} deliberately
constrained the encoder to isolate the contribution of the
attention mechanism, whereas MM-RSVQA aims to establish a
strong baseline over all three modalities, making a more
capable VHR encoder appropriate.

\textbf{Multispectral and SAR encoders.} Visual features
$f_\text{MS}$ and $f_\text{SAR}$ are extracted from
$p_\text{MS}$ and $p_\text{SAR}$ respectively using two
separate ResNet-50 models pre-trained on
BigEarthNet-MM~\cite{sumbul2021bigearthnetmm}, a
large-scale multimodal archive of paired Sentinel-1 and
Sentinel-2 patches annotated with CORINE Land Cover labels.
BigEarthNet-MM pre-training is particularly appropriate
here: it exposes the encoders to the precise spectral and
polarimetric characteristics of Sentinel-2 and Sentinel-1
data respectively, providing a much stronger initialization
than ImageNet pre-training would for these non-RGB inputs.
The patch size parameters are set to $L_\text{MS} = 100$
and $L_\text{SAR} = 200$ pixels respectively.

All three visual encoders are kept frozen during training,
ensuring that the training signal is directed toward the
fusion module rather than toward refining the visual
representations. This also stabilizes training given the
relatively small number of epochs used.

\textbf{Textual features.} The question $q$ is tokenized
using DistilBERT~\cite{sanh2019distilbert}, producing a
sequence of token embeddings $f_q$. Unlike the VHR and
multimodal visual encoders, the text encoder is fine-tuned
as part of the VisualBERT fusion module described below.

\subsection{Transformer-Based Data Fusion}
\label{sec:chap5_method_fusion}

The three visual feature vectors $f_\text{VHR}$,
$f_\text{MS}$, and $f_\text{SAR}$ are each projected to a
shared 768-dimensional space through separate linear layers.
The three projected features are concatenated with a
\texttt{[SEP]} token, which marks the boundary between
visual and textual tokens, and then with the textual token
embeddings $f_q$. A \texttt{[CLS]} token is prepended to
represent the entire input sequence.

This combined sequence is passed through
VisualBERT~\cite{li2019visualbert}, a vision-language
model based on a stack of transformer layers, pre-trained
on image-caption pairs using image-text matching and masked
language modeling objectives. VisualBERT uses self-attention
to jointly model interactions between visual tokens and
textual tokens, learning to align image regions with
corresponding words and phrases in the question. This
allows the fusion process to be \textit{trainable} and
\textit{end-to-end}: rather than fusing modalities through
a fixed rule such as concatenation or element-wise
multiplication, VisualBERT learns to weight the
contribution of each visual token dynamically, depending on
the question content and the visual context.

The key advantage of this design for the multimodal RSVQA
task is that VisualBERT can in principle learn to attend
selectively to the modality most informative for a given
question type, for instance attending primarily to VHR
tokens for fine-grained spatial questions, and to MS or SAR
tokens for broader context questions about land cover or
administrative classification.

\subsection{Prediction and Training}
\label{sec:chap5_method_prediction}

The \texttt{[CLS]} token output of VisualBERT, which
represents the fused multimodal representation, is passed
through a linear layer that maps it to a $k$-dimensional
output, where $k = 1{,}000$ corresponds to the most
frequent answers in the training set (covering 86.6\% of
all training answers). The VQA task is framed as a
classification problem over this closed vocabulary.

The model is trained using a cross-entropy loss, optimized
with Adam at a learning rate of $3 \times 10^{-5}$, a
batch size of 80, and for 5 epochs. Only questions whose
answer falls within the top-$k$ vocabulary are used during
training; the full test set is used for evaluation,
including questions whose answers lie outside the vocabulary.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:chap5_setup}

All models are trained on the training split of TAMMI
as described in Section~\ref{sec:chap5_splitting}, with
hyperparameters tuned on the validation split. Evaluation
is performed on the test split using three metrics: the
per-question-type accuracy, the overall accuracy (OA),
and the average accuracy (AA), computed as described in
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}. The full test set is
used regardless of whether a question's answer falls
within the top-$k$ training vocabulary.

Six model configurations are evaluated: the full MM-RSVQA
model using all three modalities (VHR, MS, SAR), and five
ablations examining single-modality and pairwise
combinations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{VHR only}: only the VHR orthophoto is
    used;
    \item \textbf{MS RGB + VHR}: only the three visible
    bands (RGB) of the Sentinel-2 patch are used alongside
    VHR;
    \item \textbf{MS + VHR}: all 10 Sentinel-2 bands are
    used alongside VHR;
    \item \textbf{SAR + VHR}: only the SAR patch is used
    alongside VHR;
    \item \textbf{MS + SAR}: both Sentinel-2 and Sentinel-1
    patches are used, without VHR.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sec:chap5_discussion}

\textbf{Benefit of multimodal fusion.}
MM-RSVQA (all three modalities) achieves the highest
average accuracy (AA = 65.56\%) and overall accuracy
(OA = 55.11\%) among all configurations. The gain over
VHR-only (AA = 61.94\%, OA = 52.22\%) is approximately
3.6 points in AA and 2.9 in OA, confirming the core
hypothesis of this work: even when questions concern
only the geographic extent of the VHR patch, broader
contextual information from MS and SAR modalities
measurably improves performance. This effect is most
pronounced for classification question types that
inherently require information at a broader spatial
scale, notably Urban (+24.6 points over VHR-only),
Department (+9.2), Flood Level (+5.2), and Water (+8.3),
where the context provided by Sentinel-2 and
Sentinel-1 patches is directly informative about the
classification label.

\textbf{MS RGB vs. full MS.}
The comparison between MS RGB + VHR and MS + VHR
reveals a small but consistent advantage for the
10-band multispectral configuration over the RGB-only
subset across most question types (AA: 64.72 vs.
64.87). This finding has an important methodological
implication: it empirically validates the design
choice of other RSVQA datasets (RSVQA-LR, RSVQAxBEN)
that considered only the RGB channels of Sentinel-2,
showing that the additional spectral bands provide
limited benefit for VQA at this scale. To our
knowledge, this is the first time this hypothesis has
been experimentally tested in the context of RSVQA.

\textbf{SAR contribution.}
SAR + VHR achieves performance comparable to MS + VHR
(AA: 64.40 vs. 64.87), indicating that SAR provides
contextual information of similar utility to
multispectral data for the overall task. SAR shows a
particular advantage for land cover classification
(74.89\%, the highest among all configurations),
consistent with SAR's known sensitivity to surface
roughness and dielectric properties that help
discriminate land cover classes. This suggests that
SAR and MS carry partially complementary information,
which is further supported by the MS + SAR
configuration (without VHR) achieving the second
highest AA overall (65.15\%), only marginally below
MM-RSVQA despite lacking the fine-grained spatial
detail of the VHR orthophoto.

\textbf{Challenging question types.}
Several question types remain difficult across all
configurations: Distance (9.08\%), Area (10.28--10.33\%),
Absolute Location (16.89--17.21\%), and Relative
Location (17.62--18.17\%). These are precisely the
types requiring precise spatial reasoning or metric
estimation, tasks that are inherently challenging
for a classification-based model operating on fixed
feature representations. Notably, the addition of
MS and SAR modalities does not consistently help for
these types, and in some cases (Relative Location,
Nearest) the VHR-only or SAR+VHR configurations
slightly outperform MM-RSVQA. This suggests that
the extra parameters introduced when adding modalities
require more training data to be effective for
spatially complex tasks, a hypothesis noted in the
paper and left for future work.

\section{Explored Direction: Segmentation-Guided Attention
for Multimodal Fusion}
\label{sec:chap5_seg_attention_multimodal}
% ============================================================

%A thesis allows reporting not only the contributions that
%succeeded but also directions that were explored and
%abandoned, as these carry methodological lessons that
%are not visible in the final published results. 
This section describes an earlier version of the MM-RSVQA
architecture that incorporated the segmentation-guided
attention mechanism of Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}
into the multimodal setting, and analyses why it was
ultimately replaced by the VisualBERT-based fusion
described in Section~\ref{sec:chap5_method}.

\subsection{Architecture}
\label{sec:chap5_seg_arch}

The earlier architecture retained the segmentation-guided
attention mechanism of Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}
and attempted to extend it to all three modalities.
The key design choices were as follows.

\textbf{Feature extraction.} A ResNet-50 pretrained on
ImageNet was used for all three modalities (VHR, MS,
and SAR) without the BigEarthNet pretraining that was
later adopted for the MS and SAR encoders. The MS patch
used only the three RGB bands of Sentinel-2, discarding
the seven additional spectral bands. Each encoder produced
a 1,000-dimensional feature vector ($f_\text{VHR}$,
$f_\text{MS}$, $f_\text{SAR}$).

\textbf{Segmentation-guided attention.} As in
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, a UPerNet model with a
Swin Transformer backbone, pre-trained on ADE20K and
fine-tuned on the 16-class BD TOPO annotations, was used
to compute multi-channel segmentation maps from the VHR
patch. The attention weights were computed by projecting
$f_q$ to a 250-dimensional space via a linear layer and
the segmentation features to 250 channels via a $1 \times
1$ convolution, concatenating the two representations
spatially, and applying a ReLU followed by a $1 \times 1$
convolution. Unlike Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention},
which produced a single attention glimpse, this version
produced \textit{two} attention glimpses.

\textbf{Attention transfer to MS and SAR.} The attention
map computed at VHR resolution was then \textit{downscaled}
to the spatial extent corresponding to the MS and SAR
patches, and applied to $f_\text{MS}$ and $f_\text{SAR}$
respectively. For regions of the MS and SAR patches falling
outside the VHR extent, the attention was padded with the
spatial average of the VHR attention map.

\textbf{Prediction.} The attended features from all three
modalities were concatenated with $f_q$ and passed through
a 2-layer MLP to predict the answer over the top-1,000
vocabulary, with dropout $d = 0.5$ and batch size 8.

Figure~\ref{fig:chap5_early_arch}.

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:chap5_seg_analysis}

The failure of segmentation-guided attention in the
multimodal setting cannot be attributed to a single cause.
Rather, it reflects a convergence of several compounding
issues, which we analyse below.

\textbf{Spatial resolution mismatch in attention
transfer.} The most fundamental issue is the assumption
that VHR segmentation attention can be meaningfully
transferred to MS and SAR features by spatial downscaling.
In Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, the segmentation map
and the visual features it guides are spatially aligned at
20\,cm resolution: each segmentation channel directly
corresponds to the same spatial location in the VHR
feature map, making attention a precise, pixel-level
operation. When the same attention map is downscaled to
the MS spatial extent (10--20\,m/pixel) and applied to
MS features, this spatial correspondence is lost: a single
MS feature vector aggregates information over an area
$50\times$ to $100\times$ larger than the corresponding
VHR attention cell. The downscaled attention map is
therefore too coarse to provide meaningful spatial
guidance over MS features, and the average-padding
strategy used for regions outside the VHR extent adds
further noise for the large portion of the MS/SAR patch
that lies beyond the primary area of interest.

\textbf{Inadequate encoder pretraining for MS and SAR.}
In this earlier architecture, all three visual encoders
use ResNet-50 pretrained on ImageNet, which was designed
for natural RGB images. The MS encoder receives only the
three RGB bands of Sentinel-2, discarding seven spectral
bands that carry most of Sentinel-2's discriminative
power. The SAR encoder applies ImageNet weights to
backscatter amplitude values whose statistical
distribution differs fundamentally from that of natural
images. As a result, the MS and SAR feature maps
$f_\text{MS}$ and $f_\text{SAR}$ are poorly conditioned:
they do not represent semantically meaningful spatial
structure that the segmentation-guided attention could
exploit. This contrasts with the final MM-RSVQA model,
where dedicated BigEarthNet-pretrained ResNet-50 encoders
produce semantically richer representations of the
satellite modalities.

\textbf{Dataset size relative to model complexity.}
The earlier model is trained on the partial TAMMI dataset
(146,848 Q\&A pairs over 16,274 image triplets)
while simultaneously supervising a segmentation auxiliary
task and learning attention weights for three modalities.
This represents a significantly higher number of
trainable parameters per training sample than in
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention}, where segmentation
guided attention for a single modality. The final
MM-RSVQA model, trained on the full TAMMI dataset with
3,162,514 Q\&A pairs (more than $20\times$ larger), is
better positioned to support the additional parameters
introduced by multimodal fusion.

\textbf{RGB-only multispectral input.} Using only the
RGB channels of Sentinel-2 largely negates the spectral
advantage of multispectral imagery. Most of Sentinel-2's
discriminative power for land cover and material
classification lies in the NIR and SWIR bands, which are
absent from the RGB-only configuration. This reduces the
MS modality to a lower-resolution version of an RGB
image, providing little complementary information beyond
the VHR patch and limiting the potential benefit of the
multimodal architecture.

\textbf{Lessons learned.} Taken together, these findings
motivated three key changes in the final MM-RSVQA model.
First, the segmentation-guided attention mechanism was
dropped entirely in favour of VisualBERT's trainable
transformer-based fusion, which does not require spatial
alignment between modalities and learns cross-modal
interactions end-to-end. Second, dedicated
BigEarthNet-pretrained encoders were adopted for MS and
SAR, providing semantically appropriate feature
representations for each modality. Third, 10
spectral bands of Sentinel-2 were used, and the dataset
was extended to 6 departments and 3.1 million Q\&A pairs
to provide sufficient supervision for the multimodal
fusion parameters. The improvement from AA = 44.41\% to
AA = 65.56\% between the earlier architecture and the
final MM-RSVQA model reflects the cumulative effect of
these corrections.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:chap5_conclusion}

This chapter introduced two contributions to the
multimodal RSVQA task. The TAMMI dataset combines VHR
optical imagery, multispectral data, and SAR imagery
across six departments and three contrasting geographic
regions of Metropolitan France, with 21 question types
drawn from five geographic databases and over 3.1 million
question-answer pairs. The MM-RSVQA model provides a
baseline for this dataset by fusing features from all
three modalities and the question through a VisualBERT
transformer, demonstrating consistent gains from
multimodal fusion over single-modality VHR baselines,
particularly for classification questions requiring
broader spatial context.

\textbf{Limitations.}
Several limitations of this work warrant acknowledgment.
First, the model uses frozen visual encoders; end-to-end
fine-tuning of the feature extractors, particularly the
BigEarthNet-pretrained encoders for MS and SAR, may yield
further improvements. Second, the MS and SAR patch sizes
($L_\text{MS} = 100$, $L_\text{SAR} = 200$) are fixed
hyperparameters chosen empirically; a dynamic context
selection mechanism could allow the model to adaptively
choose the spatial extent most informative for each
question. Third, questions about spatial relationships and
metric quantities (distance, area, absolute and relative
location) remain challenging regardless of the modality
combination used, suggesting that the fusion architecture
alone is insufficient for spatially complex reasoning,
a limitation shared with the model of
Chapter~\ref{chap:seg_attention} and likely requiring
dedicated spatial reasoning components. Fourth, the
dataset covers Metropolitan France only; whether the
learned representations and the question taxonomy
generalize to other geographic contexts, imaging
conditions, or sensor configurations remains an open
question. Fifth, the training vocabulary covers 86.6\%
of training answers; questions with answers outside this
vocabulary are systematically unanswerable by the model,
which penalizes overall accuracy disproportionately for
rare-answer question types such as Distance and Area.

\textbf{Perspectives.}
The TAMMI dataset and the findings of this chapter open
several directions for future work. The dynamic context
selection enabled by the configurable $L_\text{MS}$ and
$L_\text{SAR}$ parameters could be used to study how
the optimal spatial extent varies across question types.
The multi-task setting, in which a model is trained
jointly on multiple question types from the same dataset,
is a natural next step, explored in
Chapter~\ref{chap:multitask}. More broadly, the
combination of rich geographic database annotations and
three complementary image modalities makes TAMMI a
suitable testbed for future work on multimodal RS
foundation models.

The results of this chapter confirm that multimodal
fusion improves RSVQA performance when questions require
broader spatial context. The following chapter builds
on this dataset to investigate whether jointly training
on multiple complementary tasks can further improve the
model's ability to answer complex questions about
remote sensing scenes.
